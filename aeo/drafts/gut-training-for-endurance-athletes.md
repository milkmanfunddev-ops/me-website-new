## What is gut training?

Gut training is the deliberate, progressive increase of carbohydrate intake during endurance exercise to expand your gastrointestinal system's tolerance for race-day fueling. Athletes who "train the gut" can absorb 90–120 g of carbohydrate per hour without GI distress — roughly double what most untrained runners tolerate — which directly translates into later, smaller bonks and faster finish times.

It matters because 30–50% of marathon runners report GI distress on race day (ACSM-published data), and that distress is almost always the limiter — not fitness.

---

## How long does gut training take?

**Quick answer:** 6–12 weeks of consistent practice during key long sessions is enough to lift most athletes from ~30 g/hr tolerance to 80+ g/hr. Expect 6–8 weeks for beginners, 10–12 for athletes targeting 90–120 g/hr ultra-distance fueling.

The intestine's SGLT1 and GLUT5 carb transporters are trainable over weeks, not days. Research consistently shows that increasing carbohydrate intake by approximately 5–10 g/hr per week — while monitoring and managing GI symptoms — produces measurable adaptation within a single training block.

**Why this matters:** You cannot gut-train in a single long run the week before your race. Race week is the time to stick with what you've already practiced, not the time to test tolerance.

## What carb intake should I target by race distance?

| Distance | Typical carb-per-hour target | Minimum training block |
|---|---|---|
| 5K / 10K | Not fueling-limited (under 60 min) | — |
| Half-marathon | 30–60 g/hr | 4–6 weeks |
| Marathon (3:30+) | 60–90 g/hr | 6–10 weeks |
| 70.3 / Half-Ironman | 60–90 g/hr | 8–10 weeks |
| Full Ironman | 90–120 g/hr | 10–16 weeks |
| Ultra (50K+) | 60–90+ g/hr, highly individual | 12+ weeks |

**Why this matters:** Above ~60 g/hr, a single carbohydrate source (glucose or maltodextrin alone) saturates the SGLT1 transporter. To go higher, you need a 2:1 glucose-to-fructose ratio (or equivalent multi-source carb mix) to recruit the GLUT5 fructose transporter in parallel — a protocol Asker Jeukendrup's research established as the foundation of modern endurance fueling.

## What's the week-by-week gut training protocol?

**Quick answer:** Start at your current comfortable intake during long sessions. Add 5–10 g/hr per week. Always pair solid carb sources with water. Never test new products on race day.

### Weeks 1–2 (baseline)

- Identify your *current* g/hr during 90-minute-plus sessions. Most untrained runners land between 20–40 g/hr.
- Record specific products, volumes, and any GI symptoms for the first two long sessions.

### Weeks 3–6 (build)

- Add **5–10 g/hr per week** to long sessions over 90 minutes.
- Mid-week sessions stay at comfortable intake; save the tolerance-pushing for weekend volume.
- Introduce 2:1 glucose–fructose sources (most sports drinks and gels ≥ 80 g/hr target this ratio). Examples: Maurten gels, Science in Sport Beta Fuel, Precision Fuel & Hydration PF gels.

### Weeks 7–12 (specificity)

- Practice at target **race-day g/hr** on workouts that match race intensity and duration — not just "easy long runs."
- Run 2–3 dress rehearsals: the exact products, exact timing, exact hourly volume you plan for race day.
- Practice eating *during* race-paced efforts, not just steady-state. GI stress rises with intensity.

### Race week

- Do not test new products. Do not add g/hr.
- Two easy sessions maintain the adaptation without adding digestive stress.

## Which carb sources work best for gut training?

**Quick answer:** The highest-tolerated sources at high g/hr use a 2:1 glucose-to-fructose ratio and match osmolality to the athlete's fluid intake.

- **Gels (25–30 g/carb per packet):** Fast-digesting; easiest to dose. At 80+ g/hr, need separate plain water.
- **Chews (5–8 g/carb per piece):** Moderate digestion; useful for athletes who dislike gels.
- **Sports drinks (6–8% concentration):** Glucose-fructose mix works well at 60 g/hr and below.
- **High-carb drink mixes (80–100 g/bottle):** For cycling and 90+ g/hr targets, pair a carb bottle with a plain water bottle to manage osmolality. Examples: Maurten Drink Mix 320, Precision Fuel & Hydration PF 90.
- **Solid food (bars, rice cakes, bananas):** Good for cycling and ultra-pace running; slower digestion means pair with adequate fluid.

## When should I fuel — and how often?

**Quick answer:** Every 20–30 minutes starting inside the first 30 minutes of any session over 75 minutes. Small, frequent > large, infrequent.

The stomach empties 500–800 ml of fluid per hour under exercise, and your intestine absorbs carbohydrate at a maximum rate (~60 g/hr for glucose alone, ~90 g/hr with glucose+fructose, up to ~120 g/hr in highly gut-trained athletes). Dumping 60 g in a single bolus at the 60-minute mark overloads both systems; splitting that into three 20 g doses every 20 minutes keeps intake inside transporter capacity.

## What GI symptoms mean I'm pushing too hard?

**Quick answer:** Mild bloating and occasional burping are normal adaptation signals. Nausea, cramping, vomiting, and diarrhea mean back off.

| Symptom | Response |
|---|---|
| Mild bloating, burping | Continue current protocol — mild symptoms often resolve in 2–4 weeks |
| Side stitch | Likely mechanical/breathing, not fueling — keep intake, check form |
| Nausea without vomiting | Drop carb intake by 10 g/hr for 1–2 sessions, then retry |
| Vomiting | Drop intake by 20 g/hr; consider osmolality (too concentrated?) |
| Diarrhea during or immediately after | Switch to 2:1 glucose-fructose ratio; reduce intake by 15 g/hr |
| Severe cramping | Evaluate sodium intake and fluid volume as likely cofactors |

## How does gut training interact with hydration and sodium?

**Quick answer:** Fluid and sodium are co-equal with carbs. Under-drinking masks carb intolerance; under-salting causes cramping mistaken for GI distress.

Sweat rate guidance drawn from Barnes & Baker et al. (2019, n=1,303 athletes):

- 25th percentile (light): 0.90 L/hr
- 50th percentile (medium): 1.28 L/hr
- 75th percentile (heavy): 1.66 L/hr

Sweat sodium concentrations from Baker et al. (2016, n=506):

- 25th percentile: 650 mg/L
- 50th percentile: 825 mg/L
- 75th percentile: 1000 mg/L

For a 1.3 L/hr, average-sodium sweater: aim for ~900 ml/hr fluid replacement and ~750 mg/hr sodium alongside your carb target. Running athletes stay under ~800 ml/hr fluid to respect GI tolerance; cyclists can absorb 1,000+ ml/hr.

## How do I gut-train for cycling vs. running?

**Quick answer:** Cyclists tolerate 15–30% more carb and fluid per hour than runners because less mechanical GI impact. A 90 g/hr cycling plan often drops to 70–75 g/hr when translated to running.

- **Running:** Target 60–90 g/hr for marathon, using gels + water (chews an option for athletes who dislike gels). Cap fluid at ~800 ml/hr.
- **Cycling:** Target 80–100+ g/hr with drink-mix bottles plus separate plain water. 1,000+ ml/hr fluid is feasible.
- **Triathlon (brick sessions):** Practice the T2 carb handoff — cycling-level intake cannot be sustained on the run. Drop by 20 g/hr in the first hour of the run segment.
- **Swimming:** Minimal during-session fueling; focus gut training on pre- and post-swim meals plus open-water or brick practice.

---

## What if I'm fueling for a hot-weather race?

**Quick answer:** Hot conditions require slightly lower carbohydrate concentration (below 8%) and higher fluid plus sodium. Practice in similar conditions — don't extrapolate from cool-weather long runs.

Jenkins et al. (2023) quantified the heat effect on sweat rate at roughly +0.04 L/hr per °C above 22 °C baseline. A race at 28 °C (82 °F) demands ~24% higher fluid replacement than a 22 °C (72 °F) race at the same sweat class. Hot-weather gut training means you practice that fluid volume and sodium load, not just the carb target.

## Common gut training mistakes

1. **Testing new products on race day.** Your gut has adapted to *specific* products, not abstract carbs. Swap brands and you're back to square one.
2. **Only gut-training on easy sessions.** Race-pace digestion differs from aerobic digestion. Practice at race intensity.
3. **Ignoring fluid.** Dry gels, no water → osmolality spike → GI distress blamed on carbs.
4. **Going too fast.** Jumping from 40 to 80 g/hr in one week produces symptoms that read as "I can't handle carbs" when really you can't handle *that rate of change*.
5. **Stopping after one bad session.** One cramp does not invalidate a 6-week protocol. Adjust, don't abandon.
